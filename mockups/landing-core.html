Platform thesis

Community software should feel native to modern WordPress.

Designed in the Site Editor

Community pages should be shaped with blocks, patterns, templates, and theme-aware styling.

Managed with DataViews

Members, profile fields, forms, moderation, and future records belong in modern WordPress management surfaces.

Forums by default

Structured discussion should be part of the community model, not an extra plugin puzzle.

Native surfaces

Public pages are block-native. Management is DataViews-native.

Identity

Profiles, directories, forms, and member fields are the first proof of the core community loop.

Participation

Forums, topics, replies, spaces, and notifications are planned as coherent community primitives.

Composition

Templates and patterns should let site builders shape the community without leaving WordPress.

Respectful contrast

BuddyPress and bbPress proved communities belong in WordPress.

AssemblyPress carries that idea into modern WordPress: fewer fragments, more native surfaces, and a community model built around blocks, DataViews, and the Site Editor.

  • Current: member profiles, directories, profile fields, and edit profile forms.
  • Current: REST-backed data and a DataViews-oriented admin scaffold.
  • Planned: forums, spaces, moderation queues, templates, and onboarding.

Follow the open build.

AssemblyPress is early, intentional, and built in the open for people who want WordPress to be the best place to create owned communities again.
